How it works

Attune plays carefully tuned tones so you hear a slow pulse at a chosen frequency. That pulse is the “beat” — and it is grounded in real auditory neuroscience, not wishful thinking. There is more than one way to deliver the same beat rate.

The beat is real neurophysiology

When each ear receives a slightly different pure tone, you do not hear two separate pitches. You hear a single tone that seems to swell and fade at the difference between them — for example 200 Hz left and 210 Hz right produces a 10 Hz beat. This is a binaural beat, first described in depth by Oster (1973) and studied ever since.

The mechanism is well mapped. In the brainstem’s medial superior olive, neurons act as coincidence detectors: they compare timing between the ears and phase-lock to the cycling interaural phase difference. That periodic firing travels upward through the inferior colliculus and into cortex. EEG recordings routinely pick up auditory steady-state responses and frequency-following responses at the beat frequency — your nervous system is literally tracking the pulse.

From there comes the entrainment hypothesis: rhythmic stimulation at frequency f may pull cortical oscillations toward f, nudging functional states linked to that band (relaxed alpha, alert beta, and so on). Entrainment is not automatic for every listener or protocol, but the upstream pathway — beat perception → brainstem locking → cortical modulation — is established science.

Three ways to deliver the same Hz

Binaural is the classic method, not the only one. Binaural beats require stereo headphones so each ear gets its own carrier tone. The beat emerges inside the brain.

The same target rate (10 Hz, 15 Hz, 40 Hz, …) can also be delivered other ways:

  • Binaural — left/right frequency offset; beat perceived internally. Headphones required.
  • Ambient — the same binaural pair with a soft noise bed layered underneath. Meta-analytic work suggests masking does not reduce effectiveness and may make long sessions easier to tolerate. Still headphones.
  • Isochronic — a single tone switched on and off at the target rate. The rhythm is in the waveform itself, so speakers work. Recent EEG work finds isochronic stimulation can produce strong gamma-band entrainment, sometimes more robustly than binaural beats for certain frequencies.

For classic binaural beats, use Binaural or Ambient with headphones. Use Isochronic when you want an audible pulse or don’t have headphones.

Brainwave bands (the map Attune uses)

EEG research groups cortical rhythms into bands that correlate with functional states. Attune’s presets sit in the alpha-to-gamma range most often studied for wakeful focus — a practical guide for choosing a feel, not a diagnosis.

~1–4 HzDelta — deep sleep (not offered here)
~4–8 HzTheta — drowsiness, meditation, memory encoding
~8–12 HzAlpha — relaxed wakefulness, easy attention
~13–30 HzBeta — active thinking, sustained focus
~30–100 HzGamma — binding, high attention, sensory integration

What the research supports

Decades of work — from brainstem electrophysiology to modern EEG and clinical trials — make a strong cumulative case that auditory beat stimulation does something measurable in the brain, and often in how people feel and perform.

  • Entrainment is observable. EEG studies report increased power at the target beat frequency during listening — including beta (~16 Hz) and gamma (40 Hz) in recent parametric work that recorded brain activity throughout a sustained-attention task. A 2023 systematic review of 14 EEG entrainment studies found five clearly supported the hypothesis; much of the apparent disagreement traces to inconsistent carriers, durations, and analysis methods rather than a null effect.
  • Behavioral effects are real on average. A meta-analysis of 22 studies (Garcia-Argibay et al., 2019) reported a medium, significant overall effect on cognition, anxiety, and pain perception (g ≈ 0.45). Longer sessions and listening before or during a task tended to work better than brief exposure alone. Perioperative trials have found binaural audio can reduce anxiety and physiological stress markers compared with non-binaural control music.
  • Frequency and protocol matter. Alpha-range beats are often linked to calm alertness; beta and gamma beats to sustained attention. Effects depend on beat frequency, carrier tone, exposure length, and whether you are actively working or winding down — which is why Attune offers presets instead of a single “magic” frequency.
  • 40 Hz has serious research behind it. MIT’s GENUS program uses coordinated 40 Hz light and sound to restore gamma rhythms; animal studies show reduced Alzheimer’s pathology, and early human trials report neural entrainment, preserved brain volume, and exploratory cognitive benefits in some participants. Attune’s 40 Hz preset is audio only — not a medical device — but it targets a band with genuine, active neuroscience interest.
  • Rhythmic stimulation works as a broader principle. Pulsed auditory input is used in sleep research, pre-surgical anxiety protocols, and meditation studies. Binaural and isochronic beats are structured ways to deliver that rhythm at a chosen rate.

Honest limits

Steel-manning the science does not mean every claim on the internet holds up. Individual responses vary; some studies find no entrainment; expectation and context still matter. The field would benefit from more standardized protocols (carrier frequency, session length, sham controls). Attune is a focus and wind-down tool — not a treatment for any condition. Use the band names as a compass for how you want to feel, and judge by your own experience.

Why Attune softens the sound

Raw tones can feel harsh and “electronic.” Attune uses a lower carrier, fades in/out, and a Softness control that muffles the highs. Masking blends in a brown-noise texture under the beat — turn it up to bury the lab-tone feel. Motion adds a slow volume swell (about 15 seconds per breath); higher values breathe more deeply. Neither changes the target beat frequency.
