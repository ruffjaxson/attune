How it works

Attune plays carefully tuned tones so you hear a slow pulse at a chosen frequency. That pulse is the “beat.” There is more than one way to deliver the same beat rate.

Do focus tones require binaural?

No — but binaural is the classic method. “Binaural beats” specifically means two slightly different pure tones, one in each ear. Your brainstem perceives the difference (for example 200 Hz left + 210 Hz right → a 10 Hz beat). That only works with stereo headphones.

The same target rate (10 Hz, 15 Hz, 40 Hz, …) can also be delivered other ways:

  • Binaural — classic left/right frequency offset. Headphones required.
  • Ambient — the same binaural pair, plus a soft noise bed so the tones feel less stark. Still headphones.
  • Isochronic — a single tone pulsed on/off at the target rate. The beat is in the sound itself, so speakers work. It is not binaural; it is a different technique aimed at the same Hz.

If you want classic binaural beats, use Binaural or Ambient with headphones. Use Isochronic when you prefer a pulsed tone or don’t have headphones.

Brainwave bands (rough map)

EEG research groups cortical rhythms into bands. Attune’s presets sit in ranges people often associate with calm focus or alert thinking — a practical map for choosing a feel, not a medical claim.

~1–4 HzDelta — deep sleep (not offered here)
~4–8 HzTheta — drowsiness, light meditation
~8–12 HzAlpha — relaxed wakefulness, easy focus
~13–30 HzBeta — active thinking, problem-solving
~30–100 HzGamma — binding, high attention (debated)

What studies actually show

The idea that listening to a beat “entrains” your brain to that frequency is popular, but the evidence is mixed and often modest.

  • EEG work has sometimes found that binaural beats can modulate oscillatory power or connectivity, especially in alpha/beta ranges — results vary by protocol, carrier, and listener.
  • Behavioral studies on attention, anxiety, and memory report small or inconsistent effects. Meta-analyses urge caution: many trials are small, and expectation effects are hard to rule out.
  • Isochronic tones and rhythmic stimulation more generally can affect subjective arousal and attention; effects are not guaranteed for every person.
  • 40 Hz stimulation has drawn interest in Alzheimer’s research (often light and sound together in labs). Consumer audio alone is not a treatment.

A fair takeaway: these tones can be a pleasant, structured backdrop for focus or wind-down. Treat any “brain state” language as a guide for choosing a feel, not as clinical advice.

Why Attune softens the sound

Raw sine waves can feel harsh and “electronic.” Attune uses a lower carrier tone, fades in/out, low-passes the signal, and (via the Softness control) blends in a quiet pink-noise bed so the beat sits in a mufflier texture. Turn Softness up for a more padded sound; turn it down for a cleaner, more clinical tone.
